## Summary

CoDeR studies a common failure mode in retrieval systems: documents can be semantically close to a query while violating the query's local constraints. This is especially harmful for negation, exclusion, antonymy, and other constraint-sensitive search scenarios, where retrieving topically related but constraint-incompatible evidence can mislead downstream users or models.

The method keeps a standard topical retriever for broad candidate coverage, while adding a compatibility scorer that judges whether a candidate document satisfies or violates the local constraint direction of the query. This compatibility signal can rescore topical candidates or retrieve an auxiliary compatibility-oriented candidate set, improving constraint-aware ranking without calling an external LLM at inference time.

## Introduction

Modern dense retrieval often treats semantic similarity as a proxy for relevance. CoDeR argues that this proxy is incomplete: for queries with local constraints, relevance requires both topical match and constraint compatibility. By explicitly modeling compatibility, CoDeR helps retrieval systems avoid evidence that is close in topic but opposite in meaning, making retrieval more reliable for constraint-sensitive information needs.
